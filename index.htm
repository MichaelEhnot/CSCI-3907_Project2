For this project, I worked on making something called a “hybrid image”. In short, a hybrid image is an image that looks like one thing from one perspective, but also looks like something else from another perspective. In this case, the change in perspective is distance. Each image looks like one thing from up-close, and like something else from far away (You can also squint really hard to simulate being far away from the image). Using inspiration from “Hybrid Images” by Aude Oliva, I made my own hybrid images that have different pictures based on how far away they are being viewed from.

To make a hybrid image, I filtered out the “high frequency” components of one image and the “low frequency” components of the other image. In this context, low and high frequency components refer to the parts of an image where there are things like lines, borders, and changes in color. The high frequency components are areas where there is a lot of change across the pixels of an image, and low frequency components are areas where there are very little changes across each pixel.

To find high and low frequency components, I used a gaussian filter to find areas where the pixel change is high or low. To keep the low frequency components of an image, you simple apply a gaussian filter which slightly blurs the image, removing harsh changes across pixels. To keep the high frequency components, you use a gaussian filter to find the low frequency components of the image then simply subtract them from the original image, leaving the high frequency components.

Finally, you add the high frequency image to the low frequency image to make the hybrid image. Here are my hybrid images.

This is a combination of a bunch of bananas and a bunch of grapes. Both images are easy to spot, but the two images do not line up very cleanly.

 

This image is a combination of two sides of a quarter. I though it would be cool to make the coin apart to be heads from one distance and tails from another. The hybrid image is lined up well, but it is a little hard to distinguish between heads and tails. From very far away the coin looks like heads, and from up close, it looks more like tails.

 

This image combines a slice of watermelon and a slice of pizza. Probably the hardest part about making hybrid images is the selection of images. I’ve learned that the low frequency image needs rely on color and shape to be identified because the texture of the image is usually blurred away by the filter, the high frequency image should rely more on its texture to identified, because the filter usually removes its predominant colors.

 

I think this image is my favorite. From up close, you mostly see the fine details and texture of the orange, but when you move far away, you only see the orange basketball with black lines on it. Reflection

This was a very interesting project that taught me a lot a bout filtering and deconstructing images. I think that the hardest part of the project was finding the images to combine. There are many different aspects of images that can mess up a hybrid image or make the hybrid image look unrecognizable. I think the most important part of image selection is the size and shape of the objects in the image. My best results came from objects that were the same size and shape like the quarters and basketball images. I tried making hybrid images of things like birds and planes or different shoes, but the shape and colors of those images just weren’t similar enough to look good together. Both of the objects have to be pretty close to the same shape in order to make the image look nice. Its almost like the low frequency image acts as the base coat and the high frequency image is the pattern on top. If the high frequency image is not “inside” the low frequency image, it looks like there is a ghost popping out of the low frequency image. The color of the images is important too, the predominant color of the image will be the color of the low frequency image and if the high frequency image is different color than that, the low frequency image won’t have the right color. Another difficult aspect of making hybrid images is finding the right threshold frequency of the high and low frequency filters. You can change how much a gaussian filter “blurs” an image and for each image you have to find a happy medium for each filter, so each image is clearly visible at distinct distances.

In the future, it may be useful to take my own images to make hybrid images. This would allow me to stage the objects in the exact way that would make a nice hybrid image. However, it would still be difficult to know exactly how the hybrid image would appear even while taking the pictures.
